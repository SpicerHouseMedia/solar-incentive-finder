Why New York Is a Top Solar State

New York might not sound like a solar paradise — it's cold, it snows, and the sun isn't shining like in Arizona. But don't let the weather fool you.

New York is actually one of the best states in the country for solar. Here's why:

  • Electricity rates in New York are some of the highest in the U.S. — meaning every solar dollar saved goes extra far.
  • The state offers a 25% tax credit on top of the federal 30% credit.
  • NY-Sun gives cash incentives based on system size.
  • Net metering gives you full credit for the power you send back.
  • Property taxes and sales taxes on solar equipment are exempt.

When you stack all of these together, the savings can be massive. A system that costs $30,000 might end up costing less than $15,000 after incentives. That's real money.

Federal

30% Federal Tax Credit (ITC)

The federal Investment Tax Credit gives you 30% of your total system cost back as a credit on your federal income taxes. This includes panels, inverters, batteries, and installation labor.

The 30% rate is locked in through 2032 thanks to the Inflation Reduction Act. After that, it drops to 26% in 2033 and 22% in 2034. So there is no rush — but locking in the full 30% sooner means more savings.

Example: A $28,000 system gives you a $8,400 federal tax credit.

30% Federal Credit Through 2032 Applies to panels, batteries, labor
New York State

NY State Solar Tax Credit

New York offers its own solar tax credit of 25% of system cost, capped at $5,000 — whichever is less.

This one is easy to understand:

  • If your system costs $10,000, you get $2,500 back.
  • If your system costs $30,000 (25% = $7,500), you get the $5,000 cap instead.

You can claim this credit alongside the 30% federal credit. They stack on top of each other.

$5,000 Maximum NY State Credit 25% of cost, whichever is less
NY-Sun Program

NY-Sun / MW Block Incentive

The NY-Sun program is New York's flagship solar incentive. It pays you per watt of solar capacity you install.

It works in "MW Blocks" — chunks of total solar capacity allocated for each region. As more solar gets installed, the incentive per watt drops. This is called a declining block structure.

How it works:

  • Earlier blocks pay higher rates per watt.
  • Later blocks pay less as the program fills up.
  • The incentive is paid directly to your installer, which lowers your upfront cost.
  • You don't have to apply — your solar installer handles it.

Current incentives range from roughly $0.20 to $1.10+ per watt depending on your region and which block is active. A typical 8 kW system in a higher-rate block could get an extra $8,800+ incentive.

How MW Blocks & Income Levels Work

The NY-Sun program divides the state into regions, each with their own MW (megawatt) blocks. Each block covers a certain amount of total installed solar capacity in that area.

Within each block, the incentive rate depends on two things:

  • Your region — areas with less solar historically (like upstate NY) tend to get higher incentive rates to encourage growth.
  • Your income level — low- and moderate-income households qualify for higher incentives within each block, sometimes up to 20-40% more per watt.

Here's a simple way to think about it:

Block 1 (early adopters): $1.10/watt

Block 2: $0.85/watt

Block 3: $0.60/watt

Rates vary by region — these are examples to show how they decline.

The key point is: the sooner you go solar, the higher the block incentive. Once a block fills up, the rate drops. Your installer will check what block is currently active in your area.

⚡ Net Metering in New York

New York has some of the best net metering rules in the country.

When your solar panels produce more power than you use (like on sunny afternoons), the extra goes back to the grid. Your utility then gives you a credit — at the full retail rate — for that electricity.

Later, when your panels aren't producing (like at night or on cloudy days), you can use those credits to offset your bill. It's like a bank account for your solar energy.

Important details:

  • Credits roll over month to month, up to the end of each annual billing cycle.
  • Systems up to 25 kW (for homes) are eligible.
  • Net metering is available from all investor-owned utilities in New York.

New York State

Property Tax Exemption for Solar

Adding solar panels increases your home's value — but in New York, it won't increase your property taxes.

The state offers a 15-year exemption for most areas. If you live in New York City, it's 20 years.

That means for the full period, the extra value your solar panels add to your home will not be counted in your property tax assessment. You get the home value bump without the extra tax bill.

15-20 yrs Property Tax Exemption 15 years statewide, 20 years NYC
New York State

Sales Tax Exemption on Solar Equipment

New York removes the state sales tax on solar equipment. If you'd normally pay 8.875% sales tax on a $30,000 system, that's a savings of over $2,600 right off the bat.

Local tax rates vary by area, but the exemption applies statewide. Some localities also exempt solar equipment from local sales taxes.

Utility Programs by Company

New York has multiple utilities serving different parts of the state. Here's what each major utility offers for solar customers:

🏢 Con Edison (ConEd)

Serves New York City and Westchester. Con Edison offers net metering at full retail rate and participates in the NY-Sun MW Block program. They also have community solar programs and specific rebate tiers for residential customers.

🏢 National Grid

Serves parts of upstate New York including the Hudson Valley, Albany, and Syracuse areas. National Grid participates in the NY-Sun incentive program and offers net metering for systems up to 25 kW.

🏢 NYSEG

New York State Electric & Gas serves the Southern Tier and Finger Lakes region (Ithaca, Binghamton, Elmira). They participate in NY-Sun MW Block incentives, offer full retail net metering, and have interconnection rules for residential solar.

🏢 RG&E

Rochester Gas & Electric serves the greater Rochester area. Like the others, RG&E participates in NY-Sun incentives and offers full net metering for residential solar customers.

🏢 PSEG Long Island

Long Island has its own utility with high electricity rates, making solar especially attractive. PSEG LI offers net metering and participates in the NY-Sun program. They also run specific Solar Pioneer Rebate programs at times with additional incentives for the first wave of customers.

Why High Electricity Rates Make Solar So Worth It

New York has some of the highest electricity rates in the country. As of early 2026, the average residential rate in New York was around 23–28 cents per kWh — almost double the national average.

Long Island and parts of NYC can see rates over 30¢ per kWh during summer peaks.

Here's why this matters for solar:

  • The more you pay per kWh, the faster solar pays for itself.
  • Every solar kWh you produce is worth more in New York than in most states.
  • Net metering credits you at the same high retail rate you would have paid.

Bottom line: New York's high electricity costs are actually a huge advantage for solar ROI.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does solar work in New York with all the snow and clouds?

Yes. Solar panels work in any daylight — not just direct sunlight. New York gets about 1,400-1,700 peak sun hours per year, which is plenty. And snow actually helps — when it slides off, it cleans the panels for you. Plus, most solar production happens during the long summer days, which balances out the shorter winter days.

Can I stack the federal and New York solar tax credits?

Yes! They are completely separate. You can claim the 30% federal credit AND the 25% New York state credit (up to $5,000) in the same tax year. Most homeowners use both.

How long does it take to get solar permits in New York?

New York has a law called the NY Solar Bill of Rights that requires municipalities to process solar permits within 30 days for most residential installations. In practice, the whole process from signing a contract to turning on your system usually takes 2-4 months.

What if I don't have enough roof space for solar?

You have two options. First, community solar lets you buy into a shared solar farm elsewhere and earn credits on your electric bill — no roof needed. Second, if you do have room for a battery, some NY programs offer additional incentives for battery storage systems. Your installer can help you figure out the best setup.

Will the NY-Sun incentive run out?

Yes, the incentive does decline over time because of the MW block structure. More solar means lower per-watt payments. However, the state periodically adds new funding and opens fresh capacity blocks. The best strategy is to get solar sooner rather than later to lock in the highest block rate. Check with your installer for the current block in your area.
