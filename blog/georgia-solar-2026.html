Georgia is one of the sunniest states in the Southeast. The Peach State gets about 4.5 to 5 peak sun hours per day on average. That makes it a great place for solar panels — even without a lot of state-level incentives.

The truth is, Georgia's state programs are pretty thin compared to places like California or New York. But the federal tax credit is still huge, and Georgia Power has made some progress on solar buy-back. Here is exactly what is on the table for Georgia homeowners in 2026.

30% Federal Tax Credit (ITC)

If your solar system costs $30,000, you get $9,000 back from the IRS. It's a dollar-for-dollar credit on your federal income taxes. This is the single biggest financial benefit for Georgia solar buyers, and it is valid through 2032.

You can claim 30% of the total system cost — including panels, inverters, labor, and even battery storage if installed at the same time.

Georgians Should Know: No State Solar Tax Credit

Here is the honest truth — Georgia does not have a state-level solar tax credit. This is unlike many other states that layer their own credit on top of the federal one. Some Georgia lawmakers have pushed for solar tax incentives in the past, but as of 2026, nothing has passed into law.

The good news? The 30% federal ITC is still one of the largest solar incentives in the country — even without any state help.

Georgia Power Net Metering Pilot

Georgia Power runs a net metering pilot program that lets rooftop solar owners sell excess energy back to the grid. But it comes with a catch — the program has strict capacity caps and enrollment limits.

Even if you are accepted, compensation rates may be lower than the retail price of electricity. This is unlike states with full 1:1 net metering, where every watt you send to the grid gets credited at the same rate you buy power.

Bottom line: Ask your installer about Georgia Power's current net metering policy before you sign any contract.

Property Tax Exemption for Solar — Varies by County

Georgia has no statewide property tax exemption for solar installations. However, some individual counties have chosen to exempt the added home value from solar panels from property tax calculations.

This means the extra value your home gets from solar may not raise your property tax bill — but only in certain counties. Other counties will tax that added value like any other home improvement.

Check with your local county tax assessor to find out if your area offers an exemption.

Georgia Power Renewable Energy Programs

Beyond net metering, Georgia Power offers a few other programs that can help solar owners and green energy buyers:

  • Community Solar: Georgia has a community solar program. If your roof cannot handle solar panels, you can subscribe to a shared solar farm near you and get credits on your bill.
  • Green Energy Program: Georgia Power lets customers choose to pay a small premium to support renewable energy generation.
  • Electric Vehicle Programs: Georgia Power has made EV charging cheaper. Pair an EV with rooftop solar for maximum savings.

💡 Tip: Contact Georgia Power directly or ask your solar installer about all current utility programs. Rates and eligibility change often.

How Georgia Net Metering Comparest to Other States

If you have looked into solar in other states, you may hear a lot about net metering. Net metering is when your utility company buys back the extra electricity your panels produce, and gives you a credit on your bill.

In many states, net metering works at a 1:1 ratio. That means for every kilowatt-hour (kWh) you send to the grid, you get a credit for one kWh. Georgia is not quite there yet.

Georgia's net metering pilot program has limitations:

  • The total program capacity is capped — once full, new applicants cannot join.
  • Compensation rates may be based on avoided cost rather than retail electricity rates, which means you get paid less per kWh.
  • Not all Georgia utilities participate in the program.

This is one reason why battery storage is becoming more popular in Georgia. If you cannot send excess power back to the grid at a good rate, it makes sense to store that energy in a battery and use it at night instead.

How Long Until Solar Pays for Itself in Georgia?

Most Georgia homeowners see a payback period of 8 to 13 years. Here is what drives that timeline:

  • System size and cost: Bigger systems cost more but save more. Find the right balance.
  • Electricity rates: Georgia's average rate is around 12–14 cents per kWh. Higher rates = faster payback.
  • Net metering participation: If you are in the Georgia Power pilot program, your payback is shorter because you get credit for excess energy.
  • Sun exposure: A south-facing roof with no shade will produce way more energy than a shaded or east-facing roof.

Solar panels last 25 to 30 years. So even on the longer end (13 years payback), you still get 12 to 17 years of essentially free electricity after that.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Georgia offer a state solar tax credit?

No. As of 2026, Georgia does not offer a state-level solar tax credit for residential solar installations. Georgia residents can still claim the 30% federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC), which is one of the biggest solar incentives available — even without any state-level help.

Does Georgia have net metering for solar?

Georgia has limited net metering compared to many other states. Georgia Power operates a net metering pilot program with capacity caps and enrollment limits. Even if you qualify, compensation may be lower than the retail rate. Not all Georgia utilities offer net metering. Always ask your installer about your utility's specific policy.

How much does solar cost in Georgia?

The average system runs between $2.80 and $3.50 per watt. For a standard 10 kW home system, that is roughly $28,000 to $35,000 before incentives. After the 30% federal tax credit, your net cost drops to about $19,600 to $24,500.

How long does it take for solar to pay for itself in Georgia?

Most Georgia homeowners recoup their solar investment in 8 to 13 years. The exact timeframe depends on your roof, system cost, how much sun you get, and whether you are in Georgia Power's net metering program.

Are there property tax incentives for solar in Georgia?

It depends on your county. Georgia has no statewide property tax exemption for solar. Some individual counties offer exemptions on the added home value from solar installations. You should contact your county tax assessor to find out if your specific county offers this benefit.
